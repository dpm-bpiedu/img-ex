Introduction to Learning and Assessment

I think animal testing is a terrible idea; they get all nervous and give the wrong answers.

—A Bit of Fry and Laurie

People, too, sometimes give the wrong answers on tests because they get nervous and scared. That’s what often happened to Jacob.

Tests scared the pants off him. When he sat down to take one, he would quickly forget every sensible thought and well-memorized fact he had stuffed into his brain.

His test results were a disaster.

They greatly chagrined his mother—especially when, following a series of countywide achievement tests, the principal called her in.

“Jacob should probably stop dreaming about going to university and becoming a professor,” said the principal. “There’s other stuff he can do.”

But Jacob didn’t stop dreaming. He just kept trying to educate himself. “They can’t teach me more stuff just by giving me more tests,” he declared. “That’s about as sensible as thinking you can make yourself thinner just by weighing yourself a lot.”

1.1 Introduction

Focus Question What is this chapter about?

Jacob was a bright young man (his uncle once described him as having a brain as big as a planet). But he was a victim of what psychologists label test anxiety.

The thought of any sort of assessment, and the fear that he would not do well, invariably led to an emotional state that interfered with his performance. As Jacob explained it, school assessments made him feel like a cat that’s falling with its back to the ground and it just can’t seem to get its feet pointed the right way.

Helping Students With Test Anxiety

Assessment can be like that for some students: highly threatening and anxiety provoking.

This is especially the case when the student’s performance has profound implications not only for the learner, but perhaps also for the teacher, the school, the parents, and others—a situation labeled high-stakes testing (discussed in Chapter 10).

But assessment in many of today’s schools is very different from what it was in Jacob’s school.

In fact, the best current approaches to classroom assessment are designed less to determine how well or how poorly students perform than to help them learn.

Such approaches to testing might well have uncovered Jacob’s problem and led to solutions for it before he abandoned all hope.

Looking Ahead

This chapter looks at current approaches to educational assessment and at its various purposes and forms.

It explains how assessment is driven by the main, overriding goals of the educational enterprise, how curricula are designed around these goals and how assessments are used to gauge the extent to which goals are being met.

It looks at the implications of the various pieces of legislation that direct and influence educational endeavors.

It examines some of the rules and regulations designed to ensure that assessments are as fair, useful, and ethical as possible. And it looks at what we know about approaches that work in education.

The first purpose is the most important, strongly reflected in today’s assessment literature (e.g., Stiggins & Chappuis, 2017). That purpose is emphasized throughout this text.

Summative Assessment

Assessment used mainly to provide a mid-term or year-end grade or to assess mastery at the end of a unit is called summative assessment.

Summative assessment has traditionally been the main function of testing in schools, partly because the historical emphasis has been on sorting learners to select those with the highest probability of future success.

Today, selecting students is no longer the principal mission of our schools, as Stiggins and Chappuis (2017) explain. Now schools also need to ensure that learners learn how to learn—that they become competent, self-directed learners, actively involved in their own learning.

But yesterday’s approaches to education do not work well for the school’s new mission, or for today’s students, argues Prensky (2011; 2012a; 2012b). Today’s learners are very different learners: They are members of what is sometimes labeled the net generation; Oblinger & Oblinger, 2005) (or the Z generation, the iGen, the post-millennials, or any of a variety of other competing labels). Basically, these are learners so profoundly influenced by digital technologies such as the Internet, video games, and computers that they think and learn differently from older generations of students. (We look at some characteristics of today’s learners and at their impact on learning and assessment in Chapter 3).

Because today’s learner is different, and because what we expect of our schools is also different, contemporary emphases are changing. They reflect our beliefs and intentions regarding the main purpose of schools. Today’s model is more egalitarian: It advocates success for all rather than success for the chosen few. Furthermore, educators such as Prensky (2016) argue strongly that education for the future should be less about improving individuals than about improving the world. In his words, “Kids now need an education that is far more connected and real than in the past—an education that gives them not only knowledge, but also provides them with empowerment and agency” (p. 1). Accordingly, today’s teacher views assessment mainly as a tool to provide guidance and assistance—a tool designed to make today’s student a highly competent, self-motivated, life-long learner. The hope is that this learner will also be imbued with the motivation and passion to make the world a better place.

Formative Assessment

Assessment designed to improve the teaching/learning process is formative assessment. It consists of the huge variety of methods teachers use to assess and assist learner progress and comprehension during a lesson, a course, or a unit.

Put another way, formative assessment is assessment for learning; summative assessment is assessment of learning.

Characteristics

Formative assessment is assessment that is embedded in the teaching–learning process. Much of it is informal rather than structured and formal:

  • consists of ongoing strategies such as questions, quizzes, and observation of student performances and products.
  • involves providing frequent and timely feedback aimed directly at improving teaching and learning.

Instead of summarizing achievement with a number or a grade (summative assessment), formative assessment provides information upon which teacher and learner may act before formal grading occurs.

Key to Assessment in Schools

As Hattie (2012) puts it, “The primary function of assessment is to support learning by generating feedback that students can act upon in terms of where they are going, how they are going there, and where they might go next” (p. 126). Hence, the primary purpose of assessment in schools is (or should be) formative.

Placement Assessment

As the name implies, placement assessment is designed to provide information relating to the placement of a learner. It typically occurs prior to an instructional sequence, and looks at the learner’s readiness for a lesson, a unit, a course, or an entire program.

Placement assessment often includes diagnostic assessment, which is directed specifically at identifying learner strengths or learning problems and the reasons for them.

For example, diagnostic assessment might be used to determine whether a learner would benefit from being placed in a group that receives remedial instruction in reading or mathematics.

That learners and educational emphases are changing doesn’t mean, of course, that all teachers stress the formative role of assessment more than its summative uses. Some critics point out that many of today’s schools still reflect the approaches and emphases of past centuries.

Groshell (2017), for example, notes that although there have been enormous advances in technology and in educational goals, relatively little has changed in how schools view learning and in how they design the learning environment. Still, there is little doubt that the uses and purposes of educational assessment are changing. And even less doubt that they should.

Performance-Based Assessment

Another important category of educational assessments is performance-based assessment. Performance-based assessments ask learners to demonstrate their skills, knowledge, and understanding by actually performing a task or producing a product that directly illustrates what is being assessed. Thus, performance-based assessments can be used for both formative and summative purposes.

For example, asking first-grader William to read aloud from one of the worksheets that accompanies the Beginning Reader series is performance-based assessment.

Judging William’s performance according to the rubric, a type of scoring guide described in Chapter 7 and shown in Figure 1.1, provides a basis for using the descriptor Meets Standard on his electronic report card. This would be an example of the use of performance assessment for summative purposes.

If, in the course of observing and assessing William’s reading performance, the teacher notes that he consistently confuses the letters b and d as well as p and q—a common error—she can then use this information to adjust her instructional approach and provide William with additional help. This example illustrates how performance-based assessment can be used for formative purposes.

To summarize: As Figure 1.2 illustrates, assessment might occur before instruction and might be the basis for decisions regarding placement of learners (placement assessment).

Placement assessment might also involve diagnostic assessment, the main purpose of which is to identify learner strengths and weaknesses, to diagnose learning problems, and perhaps to uncover the reasons for them.

Assessment that occurs during instruction is formative to the extent that it provides feedback to assist in the learning process. Formative assessment is an integral part of the teaching–learning process rather than something that occurs only before or after instruction.

1.4 The Curriculum Planning-Assessment-Teaching Cycle

Focus Question What are simple definitions of assessment, measurement, tests, and evaluation?

To make the best possible use of all forms of assessment in education, three fundamentally important points need to be kept in mind from the outset:

  1. Assessment is an integral part of the teaching–learning process.
  2. Assessment is not simply something that teachers do to students; it’s something that is accomplished collaboratively by learners and teachers.
  3. The most important purpose of educational assessment is to provide information to enhance student motivation and learning, and to improve instructional approaches.

Figure 1.3 illustrates these three points.

Note that although assessing, planning, and teaching are shown as distinct activities, they constantly overlap.

Assessing occurs while teaching, before teaching, and after teaching. Planning and modifying approaches to instruction and to learning—all of which rely on good assessment—are continuous, ongoing processes.

The most useful assessments for instructional purposes are informal and frequent ungraded assessments that inform both the teacher’s and the students’ minute-to-minute and day-to-day activities.

Test

In education, the word test typically refers to specific instruments or procedures designed to measure student achievement or progress, or to identify various student characteristics.

Tests typically consist of a sample of behaviors (responses) that serve as a basis for making inferences. For example, a ninth-grade algebra final test does not examine candidates’ complete body of algebraic knowledge or their ability to solve all algebraic problems related to the entire course. Instead, the test consists of a representative sample of problems. If a student responds correctly to 84 percent of the problems, the examiner infers that the student also would have responded correctly to 84 percent of the entire pool of relevant problems.

Test results are often quantitative (they provide a numerical measure such as 90 percent, or a grade with clear meaning such as a B), but sometimes they are qualitative (they describe some particular quality such as Meeting all standards, or Excellent descriptive paragraph). Tests, whether they are teacher-made or professionally produced, are a relatively formal approach to assessment. They are common in placement and summative assessment, but less common in formative assessment, which tends to make greater use of more informal assessments.

Measurement

Measurements are usually quantitative: They are used to provide an indication of amount. Thus, I measure the length of my foot in inches or centimeters; but I can test its suitability for running by putting it through a series of movements.

Measurement in education is usually based on the results of tests. Strictly speaking, educational measurement is a set of procedures for manipulating and interpreting the results of tests.

For example, educational measurement might involve decisions about specific, measurable levels of performance that indicate acceptable performance in a unit test, or that might be used for identifying learners in need of assistance.

Assessment

Assessmentis a more general term. In an educational setting, assessment refers to all the activities—including testing and measuring—undertaken by a teacher to obtain information about students’ characteristics, their progress and achievement, and the effectiveness of instructional methods in relation to desired outcomes.

There is a strong belief that in the 21st century, assessment should be a collaborative process, engaging both learner and teacher, and perhaps involving parents as well. In this sense, assessment reflects a commitment to achievement and progress, and the recognition that assessment-based feedback is an essential component of the teaching–learning cycle.

Educational assessment is no longer viewed as a mysterious, secretive, teacher-controlled process. Today’s learner should be able to access assessment-based, formative feedback in collaboration with the teacher—perhaps by means of face-to-face interactions or via online digital technology.

Evaluation

Literally, evaluation refers to the process of making a value judgment. It may involve deciding that something is good or bad, moral or immoral, effective or ineffective.

In education, however, evaluation is seldom as subjective as this definition might imply. Evaluation is usually based on the results of one or more assessments, and it often takes the form of an appraisal based on whether certain criteria have been met.

For example, qualifying for special needs services in primary grades usually requires meeting specific evaluation criteria related to speech and language difficulties, social and emotional problems, or behavioral problems.

Grade

Grades are generally numbers, letters, or verbal descriptors that describe a particular rank or quality. Thus there are different grades of beef, of lumber, of wheat, of paper—and yes, even of students.

In education, grades are summative rather than formative. They are usually based on the results of tests or other forms of assessment. Occasionally, as we see in Chapter 11, they might also reflect other factors such as performance on assignments or class participation.

What We Measure

Assessments, measurements, and evaluations are a huge part of everyday life.

You have been subjected to assessments of one kind or another all your life. In all likelihood, within a few minutes of being born, a nurse or attending physician gave you the well-known Apgar test—a test which assessed your general appearance (blue or pink), your pulse (heart rate), your grimace (reflexive reactions such as coughing, sneezing, crying, or, yes, grimacing), your activity level (muscle tone described as limp, weak, or strong), and your respiration.

The Apgar test is just one of many assessments you might have had even before you started school.

For example, perhaps your vision was tested, the condition of your teeth appraised, or your health evaluated. In many ways, life is a series of formal and informal assessments. Some show how well you are performing compared to your peers; others appraise your performance relative to some standard or norm that describes expected or average behavior or achievement.

And, of course, the preschools and schools that follow bring with them a whole range of new assessments.

At the very least, schools typically measure student achievement; most also assess various kinds of aptitude or ability. They might also examine personality, vocational and career interests, and performance in different subject areas.

How We Use Results

It is important to keep in mind that the chief purpose of all the different approaches to educational assessment is to foster learning. However, each of the broad approaches to assessment described in Table 1.3 might serve a variety of different purposes in the classroom, including:

  • Determining whether learning goals are being met
  • Assessing the effectiveness of instructional methods
  • Gauging the extent to which learners are engaged in learning
  • Monitoring student achievement
  • Providing information for parents
  • Satisfying administrative and judicial requirements
  • Appraising learner satisfaction and happiness
  • Determining student background information and readiness to learn
  • Helping learners make wise decisions
  • Developing self-assessment skills among learners
  • Making decisions about student advancement and placement
  • Advising students regarding career and vocational decisions
  • Tailoring instructional methods to student aptitudes and interests

What Information Learners Need

Most of the purposes for which educational assessment might be used are not mutually exclusive. A single well-constructed, teacher-made, end-of-unit test might serve every one of the purposes just listed.

However, the kind of assessment information the learner needs is quite different from the assessment information that might be given to parents. Interested parties such as parents, prospective employers, and university admissions officers are mainly interested in summative information that allows them to compare and evaluate. That is, they want to know what the learner has achieved relative to educational goals and relative to other learners. On that basis, they can predict what the learner is capable of, provide advice about educational and career options, and make decisions about placement.

In contrast, what learners need in the day-to-day business of learning is immediate, personalized, confidential information about their learning. They need formative information that will enhance their ongoing learning.

1.5 Educational Goals and Assessment

Focus Question What is the backward design approach to building a curriculum?

Educational assessment has an enormous influence on what students study and how they learn. In the final analysis, students try to learn what they expect teachers to test—which is, presumably, what teachers teach.

When Tests Don’t Measure Up

Unfortunately, what teachers test is not always what they claim is important.

For example, Elena’s high school English teachers, Mr. Teske, constantly emphasizes the importance of critical thinking and independent reflection. He spends hours of class time analyzing and critiquing various literary works, speculating on the moral virtues of the characters, and asking students to offer personal opinions on abstract literary matters.

But during the last class of every Friday, Mr. Teske routinely presents his learners with a fill-in-the-blank quiz consisting of questions like these:

  • The author of Wuthering Heights is _________.

  • The person who said, “Beware the ides of March” was ________.

  • Supply the missing line:

  • You blocks, you stones, you worse than senseless things!

  • O you ______________________________________

  • Knew you not Pompey?

 

Students quickly learn that if they are to do well, they should memorize as much as possible. And Mr. Teske’s main objectives—teaching learners how to think critically, to reflect, to analyze and synthesize, to evaluate—remains unrealized.

The Importance of Educational Goals (Standards)

Educational goals are the broad intentions of educational systems—the standards these systems are designed to reach. They describe what educational systems are designed to accomplish. They summarize the purposes of educational institutions and, indirectly, the goals that societies and parents have for children.

In much of the educational literature, the word standard is used interchangeably with the word goal, as it is in this text.

Read more about educational assessment terms.

Important Terms in Educational Assessment

On occasion, however, standard means a level of performance, such as in performing to a high standard. Levels of performance are often described in terms of sequential steps that form a learning progression. A learning progression is a hierarchical succession of skills and knowledge that build toward a learning objective.

The phrases learning objective and instructional objective (and sometimes educational objective) are used interchangeably: They denote the more specific objectives of the teaching/learning enterprise. A criterion in educational literature is a level of performance to which performance can be compared and evaluated. Hence success criteria denote specific criteria associated with desired or expected achievement. These important terms are defined and illustrated in Table 1.4

Educational goals might be expressed in broad terms, such as “The goal of education is to ensure that all students are taught to high academic standards.” This is one of the important educational goals of the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) (2015; 2017), which is currently the main U.S. federal law governing K–12 education.

Backward Design

Educational standards (goals) such as those described by ESSA are helpful for guiding the development of curricula and influencing the values of educators and perhaps of students and parents as well. In fact, one of the most useful approaches to designing a curriculum is to begin with the goals of that curriculum—a process labeled backward design. One of the identifying characteristics of the backward design process is that learning objectives are derived directly from educational standards.

The Process

The backward design process can be described in terms of three steps:

  1. Decide on the end results (the goals or standards) that the curriculum is intended to achieve (specify learning objectives);
  2. Clarify how you will be able to determine that the desired end results have been reached (plan for assessment);
  3. Select or design instructional activities most likely to lead to desired end results (plan teaching/learning approaches).

The Role of Assessment

Assessment plays a critical role in the backward design process. Not only are assessment decisions made even before decisions about teaching and learning activities, but, as Wright (2006) points out, assessment results can also influence instructional approaches. This might occur, for example, when formative assessments reveal that many learners are struggling to reach learning objectives.

Assessment results can also influence the goals themselves. For example, if assessments indicate that even with the most effective instructional approaches available, learning objectives remain out of reach, the objectives themselves might have to be changed.

Although the backward design model for curriculum building, assessment, and instruction is presented as a series of steps, it is, in some ways, a cycle—as is shown by the arrows in Figure 1.4.

Collaboration in the Assessment Cycle

Following passage of the ESSA, individual states are now required to develop their own educational goals (standards), to select assessments that will allow them to determine if these standards are being met, and, of course, to provide the kind of education that will maximize the likelihood of reaching these goals. As we see later in this chapter, many states have adopted common core standards developed by a national consortium of educators. And many also collaborate in developing and sharing relevant assessment and instructional materials, often through the formation of what is termed a professional learning community (PLC).

What Is a PLC?

PLCs are formal associations of educators, and sometimes of parents, who come together to collaborate and share ideas and resources relating to all things educational: goals, assessments, learning, and teaching. They often include members from different schools and sometimes even from different states. PLCs have become increasingly popular in recent decades as educational systems have faced increasing pressure to improve educational standards and student performance. They are now common worldwide and often include international collaboration (Pang and Wang, 2016).

Issues often faced by PLCs include setting and clarifying specific goals relating to the high standards most governments expect of their schools; designing curricula that incorporate these goals; developing the assessment procedures that will allow educators and administrators to monitor progress toward the goals; and devising and implementing instructional strategies most likely to provide every learner with an opportunity to reach desired standards.

How Might PLCs Use Backward Design?

The backward-design approach provides a highly useful and systematic way to attack these issues. As a result, it has become an increasingly popular approach to the cycle of designing curricula, developing assessments, and implementing teaching strategies. For example, Paesani (2017), describes how a French language curriculum was backwards-designed based on the overarching standard toward which the course was aimed, cultural literacy. By relating specific course objectives to this broad concept, explains Paesani, educators were able to devise assessment and instructional approaches that greatly increased the likelihood that learners would, in fact, develop cultural literacy relating to the French language.

Backward design approaches to specifying learning objectives, developing assessment programs, and planning instruction have also become increasingly prevalent in post-secondary institutions (for example, Reynolds and Kearns, 2017; Michael and Libarkin, 2016).

How Do Educators Write Specific Instructional Objectives?

As we saw, educational goals often take the form of broad, general intentions of educational endeavors. They are the standards to which the ESSA refers: “ESSA requires that states establish college- and career-ready standards and maintain high expectations when assessing all students against those standards” (U.S. Department of Education, 2017).

The problem for educators is that standards, such as ESSA’s requirement that all learners achieve high English language proficiency, are not nearly as helpful in the day-to-day business of teaching as are more specific statements of learning objectives—also commonly referred to as learning targets or as instructional objectives. Learning targets describe what the teacher and the student aim to achieve. They are statements of what schools expect students to know and be able to do.

Learning objectives tell the teacher:

  • What needs to be taught
  • What needs to be assessed
  • How it will be taught and assessed

The great value of the backward design approach is that it provides a clear, step-by-step method to determining what needs to be taught, how it can be assessed, and how best to assist learners in reaching curriculum objectives.

Learning objectives often take the form of statements about what is expected of students once they have completed a lesson, a unit, or perhaps an entire course. The most useful learning objectives for teaching and assessment don’t describe a lesson or a course but rather spell out the precise behaviors or skills that students should be able to perform following instruction. For this reason, learning objectives are sometimes called behavioral objectives.

 Consider the following two examples of instructional objectives:

  1. The learner should be proficient in mathematics.
  2. The learner should be able to add two-digit numbers without using computers or other mechanical or electronic devices—and without resorting to fingers or toes.
Learning targets = instructional objectives = learning objectives = behavioral objectives

The second of these objectives is more useful than the first because it states what the student must be able to do following instruction. As a result, it offers specific guidance for instruction and for assessment.

Not only do useful instructional objectives specify what the learner should be able to do, but they also provide definite criteria for meeting goals. For example, look at these instructional objectives:

  • The learner will be able to input material using a blank keyboard.
  • On a 5-minute test using a blank keyboard, the learner will be able to input 40 words per minute with no more than 3 errors when transcribing a randomly assigned passage from Catcher in the Rye.

The second of these statements establishes clear criteria for meeting instructional goals. As a result, it is more useful than the first for suggesting instructional and assessment procedures.

Writing useful learning objectives requires specifying objectives in terms of measurable outcomes. This involves all components of the assessment cycle illustrated in Figure 1.5.

Although precise, performance-oriented approaches to instructional objectives are most useful for guiding instruction and assessment, more general statements of objectives can also be helpful. For example, Waugh and Gronlund (2013) suggest that teachers should express main objectives in general rather than specific terms. Each main objective can then be developed in terms of actual behaviors that reflect the more general objective. Examples of these actual behaviors can then be used as a basis for assessment. 1.6 The Implications of ESSA

Focus Question What is the ESSA, and what is its implications for educational assessment?

The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA), passed in December 2015, reflects the tremendous concern that the public has about the quality of American schooling.

Among other things, the ESSA sets broad educational goals for all grade levels, makes individual states accountable for student achievement, and requires states to monitor student achievement (see Table 1.5 for highlights).

The ESSA replaced the No Child Left Behind Act, or NCLB(2002) which had similar goals for children but far more stringent assessment requirements. For example, under NCLB, all students were expected to pass end-of-year tests, a clearly unrealistic expectation.

Also, NCLB required that each state develop its own tests; under ESSA, states are free to use nationally recognized tests such as the ACT and the SAT (both of which are standardized tests widely used for admission to college.)

Increased Emphasis on Common Standards

One of the general requirements mandated by ESSA is that all states develop high academic standards, provide an opportunity for all learners to reach these goals, and develop assessments to monitor the extent to which goals are being reached. One of the results of this legislation has been an increased emphasis on common standards within and even across states.

Common standards help ensure equity in education across states and prevent fragmentation of education for those students who move to new communities that might otherwise have their own unique standards—an increasingly common event in a mobile population.

The Common Core State Standards—first introduced across the country in 2009, well before the passage of ESSA in 2015—is one such example of a nationwide effort to develop standards.

Read more about how Common Core was developed.

The Development of Common Core

As part of an increasingly global society, students must compete against people from around the world for jobs. They need to have the knowledge, skills, and disposition to be successful in this changing world—skills such as those involved in collaboration, critical thinking, communication, creativity, and innovation.

As leaders across the country considered these issues, many recognized the need to move beyond individual state standards for learning. They concluded that states need to agree on common standards in education that allow all children to develop the skills and knowledge required for success in a global society, regardless of where they live.

Two organizations, the Council of Chief State School Officers (CCSSO) and the National Governors Association Center for Best Practices, spearheaded a movement to bring state leaders, administrators, teachers, and other stakeholders together to design standards that are rigorous, innovative, and focused on the most important knowledge and skills required for today’s students.

The Common Core State Standards initiative sought feedback from parents, teachers, state leaders, and the general public. The standards were finalized in 2010 and revised again in 2017 InTASC Model Core Teaching Standards, 2017). States were invited to adopt the Common Core Standards, but the choice remains voluntary.

For kindergarten through 12th grade, Common Core Standards have been developed in the disciplines of English/Language Arts and Mathmatics. These content areas were chosen due to the foundational aspect of these subjects. The Common Core Standards are focused on establishing a balance between content knowledge and thinking skills.

English/Language Arts

In English/Language Arts, the standards are organized so that reading, writing, listening and speaking, and language cross all grade levels with technology embedded in each component. Students are expected to read a variety of fiction and nonfiction genres from both print and nonprint sources and to think critically about the content of the reading. Even at the lower grade levels, they are encouraged to form opinions and support them, and to develop writing skills for different purposes. Under the Common Core Standards, students have multiple opportunities to engage in dialogue in a variety of arenas, formal and informal, as they practice both listening and speaking skills.

Mathematics

Similar to the English/Language Arts Standards, Mathematics Standards require students to employ higher levels of thinking. In a global society, it is not enough that students master procedures for doing calculations—termed procedural knowledge. In addition to procedural knowledge, they need to be able to reason mathematically, solve problems, and use logic to deduce answers. They also need to be able to communicate their mathematical thinking and justify their answers and solutions. Standards that attend to both procedural competence and conceptual understanding equip students with the ability to assess a situation, determine a plan of action, carry out that plan, and evaluate its effectiveness.

The Common Core Standards present a framework that can help unify the content knowledge and thinking skills students will need in the 21st century. As states adopt these standards, they are helping to ensure that all students have a high-quality education focused on knowledge and skills required of a contributing member of today’s society.

As of 2016, 42 states had adopted the standards, often with minor changes (Korn, Gamboa, & Polikoff, 2016). However, as more states adopt the Common Core Standards, potential obstacles are beginning to appear.

One of these has to do with the costs of changing assessment systems and reporting student progress. Older methods of assessment tended to focus on recall and recognition. But because new standards attempt to foster critical thinking and problem solving, new assessments need to be developed.

In addition, teachers who have operated with standards that focus on discrete knowledge need to develop new tools for instruction. Specifically, they must develop materials that engage students in problems that do not always have a clear, correct answer. It is yet to be determined when these issues will be addressed and who will cover the costs.

 

Use of Data Analytics

With the passage of ESSA, there is clearly less emphasis on standardized testing and on what is termed high-stakes testing—testing with potentially significant

implications (see Chapter 10). However, there is still a need for teachers, schools, and states to deliver the best educational experiences possible for their charges.

Tests serve an important function in this regard: They provide numerical data that can be analyzed, sorted, transformed, and otherwise manipulated to reveal key insights—a process that defines data analytics.

The emphasis in data analytics—as opposed to data analysis—is on using information for decision making. Data analytics in education uses statistics (see Chapter 9), computer programs, learning theories, and information from educational research in an effort to improve teaching and learning.

Applications: Making Data-Driven Decisions

Data-driven decision making is a concept gaining wide acceptance from educators, parents, and policy makers. It involves using evidence, or data, to demonstrate that a decision or action is the best among available alternatives.

Avoiding Subjective Feelings

In the past, schools have been known to operate on “gut feelings.” The teacher feels that students benefit when instruction is combined with music. The principal believes the reading program is the best for the school. The board of education thinks that eliminating recess will improve test scores because more instructional time is available.

However, with the obligation to prepare students for the 21st century and the goal for students to flourish in a global society, we can no longer “feel,” “believe,” or “think”: We need to know. With data-driven decision making, teachers conduct action research within their classes to determine if music truly does benefit students. Principals and boards of education read the research that has been done or systematically investigate a given topic so they have data on which to base their decisions.

Supplying Evidence

Consider this scenario: A local school has received its test scores from a statewide assessment of science in the third grade. The principal has shared the results and asked the third-grade team to review the data. They lay out the individual teacher’s results and start looking for patterns. They consult the assessments blueprints and determine that across the four classes, one strand from the science curriculum is problematic. All four classes did poorly on the Interrelationships in Earth/Space Systems strand.

They discuss why this might have occurred and discover that for all the other science strands, they have ample supplemental teaching materials. However, for this strand they are limited to their textbook for teaching the concepts in the state’s standards. Armed with these data, they suggest to their principal that not only do they need more materials, but they also need training to use the materials. Without data to support their request, their case would be far less convincing.

Supplying Sufficient Evidence

 However, as compelling as some evidence may be, it is important to exercise caution when employing data-driven decision making.

For example, one data set is not enough. The third-grade teachers could strengthen their case by including further evidence that the Science strand is problematic. What has been the performance of the students during the actual science unit? Do their scores on quizzes and tests reflect the same low scores seen on the statewide assessment? Do the informal checks for understanding conducted while teaching indicate confusion and misconceptions?

Quality data-driven decision making does not rely on a single data set from which to draw conclusions.

Drawing Conclusions

In addition, having the data does not does not guarantee the best decision.

In the third-grade scenario, the data point to a problem with the one Science strand. The teachers provide the evidence to their principal, hoping that she will reach the same conclusion they have—more materials and training are needed.

Instead, the principal might decide to increase the amount of instructional time for this unit. Rather than spending just two weeks on this unit, she decides teachers should spend four weeks, stealing time from the two units that surround this one in the curriculum pacing guide.

Was the decision based on data? Yes. Was it a good one? We’ll have to wait to see what the later data reveal. Data-driven decision making has the potential of anchoring decisions to evidence. However, just like with most things, potential is not always reached.

1.7 High-Stakes Testing

Focus Question What are some ways legislation has created a high-stakes learning environment?

Among other things, legislation such as the Every Student Succeeds Act, seeks to make teachers and schools more accountable. One way of doing this is to specify what the goals of education are and to lay out minimum educational standards that will be acceptable as criteria for reaching these goals. An educational standard is basically a description of a required or expected level of knowledge or performance.

Another way of encouraging schools and teachers to be more accountable is to tie funding directly to the performance of students, of schools, and, by the same token, of teachers. Educational standards described in ESSA have to do with attaining proficiency in reading and mathematics, becoming proficient in English, graduating from high school, and being taught by highly qualified teachers.

How can you tell if all students have reached the required standards? The simple answer is by using tests. As we see in Chapter 10, these tests can have very high stakes indeed, because their consequences can be life changing for students, determining whether or not they pass or fail, can or cannot enter a post-secondary program, receive or do not receive a scholarship. They can also be system changing, especially if federal funds are tied to how well students perform on these tests.

Remembering NCLB

Under the older NCLB Act, states were required to set yearly performance targets with a view to reaching certain levels of proficiency within the allotted period. Performance on high-stakes standardized tests provided information for data analytic processing, the results of which then allowed the U.S. Department of Education to determine how every public school in the country was performing. The consequences of not making what was deemed adequate yearly progress (AYP) were spelled out by the NCLB Act. These included the possibility of replacing staff, hiring outside consultants, or even transferring administrative responsibilities to outside personnel.

With the replacement of the NCLB with ESSA, the notion of AYP is no longer relevant. Although schools are still expected to be accountable and to reach certain standards, they are no longer subject to the same testing procedures or punitive possibilities as was previously the case (Walker, 2015).

Assessing Teacher Competence

Also potentially very high stakes are the tests used to assess teacher competence. A variety of tests are available including the edTPA (which stands for Teacher Performance Assessment), developed by the Stanford University Center for Assessment, Learning, and Equity (SCALE), and the Praxis™ tests created by Educational Testing Service (ETS), a nonprofit organization that develops and oversees a variety of national and international tests.

The Praxis™ tests are based on 10 basic principles that describe the skills, attitudes, and behaviors thought to be characteristic of the most effective teachers (Table 1.x). The standards of effective teachers and teaching outlined in Table 1.x were developed by a consortium of school administrators, the Council of Chief State School Officers (CCSSO), and are known as the InTASC principles. InTASC stands for Interstate Teacher Assessment and Support Consortium (InTASC model core teaching standards: A resource for state dialogue, 2017).

Many teacher-training institutions use a form of this test, the Praxis I™, as a basis for admission to their teacher-training program. It is designed to assess whether candidates have minimum levels of competence in basic skills such as reading, writing, and arithmetic.

A second form of the test, the Praxis II™, looks at subject matter knowledge and related teaching skills. The Praxis II™ is sometimes a requirement for teacher certification.

A third form of the test, the Praxis III™, may also be used to determine readiness for full teacher certification. It assesses aspects of the teachers’ actual classroom performance. Unlike the Praxis I™ and the Praxis II™, which are almost exclusively computer delivered tests, the Praxis III™ uses trained examiners who interview teachers and observe them in their classrooms. Praxis I and II™ exams are scheduled at specific times and places and require payment of fees. Yearly schedules for testing times and locations are available online (for example, Teachers Test Prep, 2018).

Remembering Race to the Top (R2T)

Announced in 2009, the Race to the Top program was a federally funded ($4.35 billion) competition for funds. It ended five years later and gave rise to considerable controversy while it was in effect.

The R2T funding program was based on the principle of teacher and school accountability. Under this program, funds were awarded to school jurisdictions based on points earned for demonstrating performance-based standards, complying with national educational standards, promoting charter schools, and encouraging the use of computers in classrooms. The points received reflected such things as the competence of teachers and principals, the effectiveness of preparation programs for teachers and principals, progress in raising school achievement, and the development of high-quality assessment procedures designed to reflect increasingly high standards.

The Race to the Top funding program was designed to bring about educational reforms in the areas of standards, assessments, teacher qualifications, and low-achieving schools.

One effect of the R2T funding program was that a large number of U.S. states adopted common standards reflecting the InTASC standards developed by the Council of Chief State School Officers; others developed their own standards. A second outcome was that many states encouraged the formation of more charter schools.

However, the Race to the Top funding program was highly controversial. Because only some states “won” this funding competition, many of those that “lost” incurred significant additional expenses without seeing any corresponding increase in educational funding. As a result, many states did not apply for R2T funding or later opted out of the program. In the end, 11 states and the District of Columbia were granted R2T funds ranging from $75 million to $700 million each.

After the funding period had expired in 2015, an evaluation of the program concluded that there had been improvements in the four areas of primary concern: higher educational standards; development of assessment procedures designed to improve learning and instruction; enhanced teacher qualifications; and measurable increases in the achievement levels of lower-performing schools (U.S. Department of Education, 2015). 1.8 The Implications of IDEA

Focus Question What is IDEA, and what is its implications for educational assessment?

With the passage of a series of federal laws revised in 2004 and culminating in what is now commonly referred to as IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, the education of children with special needs has increasingly become one of the responsibilities of regular classroom teachers. This act has had important implications for the assessment of children with special needs.

IDEA gave most of the responsibility for identifying and providing services for children with special needs to special education teachers (teachers specifically trained and charged with looking after children with special needs). But the provisions of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act, together with a series of related court decisions, have been largely instrumental in revolutionizing how schools assess and deal with exceptionality.

Among other things, the law provides important guarantees for children with exceptionalities and their parents (Center for Appropriate Dispute Resolution, 2010). The guarantees include

  • Zero rejection and mandated parent participation
  • Education in the “least restrictive environment”
  • Free and appropriate educational services
  • Fair, nondiscriminatory assessment and due process of law
  • An individualized education plan (IEP)

Although these guarantees were initially intended to apply to learners with special needs, they are increasingly being applied to all learners. As a result, it is important for all teachers and educational authorities to understand the implications of IDEA.

Zero Rejection and Mandated Parent Participation

All children, no matter how severe their disabilities, are entitled to all the provisions of the act. That is, each child is entitled to education in the least restrictive environment, to nondiscriminatory assessment, to all required educational services at no cost, and to an individualized education plan.

The guarantee of parent participation grants parents the right to participate in meaningful ways in all aspects of their child’s education including assessment, development of individual education plans, school placement, and transition from school.

Education in the Least Restrictive Environment

Certain precedent-setting legal decisions have specified that children with disabilities have the right to education in the least restrictive environment. This is usually interpreted to mean a regular classroom as opposed to one designated as a “special education” classroom. This interpretation has led directly to the practice of mainstreaming,or inclusion, of children with special needs in the regular classroom.

Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)

Children with special needs must be given optimal educational experiences at public expense. Optimal or appropriate educational experiences are typically guided by a detailed education plan developed by parents, teachers, and other specialists specifically for each child.

Fair Evaluation and Due Process

IDEA requires that the identification of children with special needs, and the detailed analysis of their needs, be based on a variety of relevant and appropriate assessment procedures. Parents and teachers are usually the first to notice that a child might have special needs. IDEA mandates that subsequent assessment be undertaken by trained professionals using nondiscriminatory procedures. One of the practical implications of this guarantee is that assessment for learners whose primary language is not English must take place in their native language.

Individualized Education Plans (IEPs)

Individualized education plans (IEPs) are written programs that are required by law for every child with special needs. They are usually prepared by a team that includes the child’s parents, regular classroom teachers, special education teachers, and one or more specialists as required. On occasion, the child might also be involved in developing an IEP.

IDEA specifies that IEPs must be written documents that contain the following:

  • A clear description of the student’s current performance and achievement
  • A list of short-range objectives and of longer term goals
  • A detailed description of the special services that will be provided for the student
  • The duration of the program
  • Assessment procedures that will be used to evaluate the extent to which goals are being met

Developing detailed IEPs can be enormously challenging and time-consuming. And carrying out the provisions of these plans—including assessing the extent to which goals are being met and modifying approaches and goals as required—can be even more challenging.

1.9 Test Fairness and the Ethics of Assessment

Focus Question What are some important ethical issues in assessment?

It was clear right from the beginning that Mr. Lowen, a 12th-grade physics teacher, didn’t know squat about physics. Yet there were many who flocked to his courses. And although most students were extremely bored with his classes, many were quite happy with the inflated grades they took home.

You see, Mr. Lowen had a cupboard full of old departmental exams, complete with answer keys. And that’s what he taught: the answers to old exam questions! Hardly a recommended or ethical procedure.

The Problem of Teaching to the Test

The phrase “teaching to the test” can have two distinct meanings. The one illustrated by Mr. Lowen, also the most common, involves the teacher’s desire to boost marks with no concern for reaching instructional objectives. The other meaning refers to instructional procedures aimed directly at achieving instructional goals, an approach also expected to result in learners doing well on assessments of related educational objectives.

According to some critics, Mr. Lowen’s approach is one of the by-products of high-stakes testing. This would be evident, for example, if Mr. Lowen takes a list of vocabulary words directly from a high-stakes state test and teaches only these words and no others to his students to guarantee that they will score well on the test. That illustrates teaching to the test in its negative sense.

In contrast, a teacher might approach vocabulary development in a reading class by helping students find and learn new words in hard-to-read texts. That would be teaching to the test in its more positive sense, although it might not guarantee test marks quite as high.

As we see in Chapter 10, when there is a great deal of pressure on teachers to have their students perform well, and when the consequences of doing so—or of not doing so—are massive, instructional and assessment decisions might suffer.

Note, however, that with the replacement of NCLB with ESSA, the use of high-stakes tests has diminished and, consequently, the pressure to “teach to the test” is no longer as urgent.

The Problem of Bias

Unfortunately, the unethical use of tests is not limited to teachers coaching their students on high-stakes tests. Another misuse involves standardized tests that are biased against certain populations and groups.

For example, there is considerable evidence that children of ethnic minorities are underrepresented in the field of giftedness (Liu and Waller, 2018). This bias has sometimes been attributed to selection procedures that depend on teacher identification of likely candidates. It may also be related to the use of standardized tests that have racial bias built into them.

Racial bias in tests may result from the fact that they were developed for majority cultural groups. But, as Shuttleworth-Edwards (2016) points out, tests may also be biased when they were developed and normed with a huge multicultural population—as might be the case for tests developed in the U. S., for example.

These tests, explains Shuttleworth-Edwards, may generally represent an entire population but, because it is multi-cultural, the tests might not be a fair measure for any one of the minority cultures that make up the population. They might be biased against some students and unduly favor others.

The adoption of common standards and assessment procedures in an increasing number of states has led many to hope that long-standing cultural differences in achievement—and perhaps in opportunity as well—may be reduced. As Worrell and Jacobson (2016) suggest, the appropriate use of test scores, as well as the use of appropriate tests, may go some distance toward that goal.

Guidelines for the Appropriate Use of Tests

There are now guidelines for teachers such as Mr. Lowen, whose assessment practices are less than ideal.

One set of guidelines is that developed by the Code of Fair Practices in Education (2004), a group made up of representatives from the American Psychological Association (APA), the American Counseling Association (ACA), the American Educational Research Association (AERA), the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association (ASHA), the National Association of School Psychologists (NASP), the National Association of Test Directors (NATD), and the National Council on Measurement in Education (NCME).

The resulting set of guidelines, called the Code of Fair Testing Practices in Education, is intended to apply to all kinds of educational assessment including paper-and-pencil tests, computer-based tests, and performance tests. It is designed to provide guidance for test developers as well as those who use tests. It lists specific recommendations with respect to the following areas:

  • Developing and selecting appropriate tests
  • Administering and scoring tests
  • Reporting and interpreting test results
  • Informing test takers

Given its importance, the code is reprinted in its entirety in Table 2.3 (Chapter 2). Much of the information in the part of the table that deals with fair testing practices for test users is especially relevant for classroom teachers and school administrators. The part that deals with the responsibilities of test developers is most relevant for professional test-development companies, although it too has implications for many kinds of teacher-made tests (discussed in Chapter 8).

Most educational and psychological organizations also have other codes that specify what ethical—and unethical—behavior is for their members (e.g., the Code of Ethics of the American Educational Research Association, 2011).

Review some ethical principles for educational assessment.

Ethical Principles for Educational Assessment

  • Some ethical principles most relevant for educational assessment include the following:
  • No testing procedure should be undertaken where there is a possibility of harming participants physically or emotionally;
  • When assessments are part of a research project, informed consent of participants is generally mandatory. That is, participants must understand the implications and possible consequences of the assessment and must be free to refuse without coercion or fear of reprisal. By definition, being a student in school or college constitutes implied consent with respect to normal educational testing procedures;
  • Steps should be taken to ensure that test results as well as individual responses remain anonymous;
  • Test records must be kept private or disclosed only within the provisions of the law;
  • Caution and good sense need to be used if the results of standardized psychological tests (intelligence or personality tests for example) are to be shared with students or parents. Given the limited reliability of many of these tests, and given the implications of the labels often attached to their results, these should not always be fully disclosed;
  • Tests need to be as valid, reliable, fair, and unbiased as possible (see Chapter 2);
  • The use of deception in psychological research is justified only when the study has significant potential value, no alternative methods are readily available, there is minimal risk to participants, and the research has been approved by an ethical review board.

Teacher Responsibilities

Without a doubt, teachers have important responsibilities relating to assessment. These include choosing or developing assessments that are appropriate for what as well as for whom they are intended.

This means that assessment instruments, procedures, and strategies need to reflect intended instructional goals and that they must also be free of cultural, social class, or religious bias.

When required, assessments must also be appropriate for learners with special needs.

Testing Fairly

As we see in Chapter 2, one of the most important characteristics of educational assessments from the student’s point of view is that they be fair—or at least seem to the learner to be fair. That chapter also describes how assessments need to be valid (their interpretations and uses must be supported by evidence), and they need to measure as accurately and consistently as possible (they need to be reliable). An important aspect of the teacher’s responsibilities with respect to assessment is to ensure that assessment procedures be as fair, valid, and reliable as possible.

Scoring and Using Results

Teachers also have key responsibilities relating to scoring, interpreting, reporting, and using the results of assessment procedures (Chapter 11). Scoring and interpreting a test should be accurate and unbiased. Further, the results of a single assessment should never be the basis for major decisions.

Communicating Results

Finally, teachers are responsible for communicating assessment results to students, parents, and administration. It is important that they understand the limitations and significance of their assessments. And it is also important that they help learners and parents interpret test results and understand their meaning.

1.10 Student Rights and Responsibilities in Assessment

Focus Question What rights do students have with regard to assessment and their education?

Students, too, have responsibilities with respect to schooling and assessment—and rights as well. Among their responsibilities are obvious requirements, such as showing up in school or examination rooms at the appointed place and time; acquiring educational materials as needed; making an honest effort to participate and learn; and being honest and ethical in the completion of assignments and in test taking, avoiding plagiarism, and other forms of cheating.

The Right to an Education

Student rights vary considerably throughout the world. As we saw earlier, ESSA and IDEA specify that in the United States, all students have the right to a “free and appropriate” education, to be taught by highly qualified teachers, and to be taught in such a way that all will reach a minimum level of proficiency in reading, writing, and arithmetic and all will graduate from high school.

Although the probability that all students will fulfill these ideals is unrealistic, that each should have a legitimate opportunity to do so is really the most important goal.

The Right to Assembly and Expression

Within certain legal limits, students in U.S. schools also have rights relating to freedom of expression and of assembly. And in the event of a grievance, they have a right to due process of law.

A declaration of school students’ rights adopted by the general assembly of the Organizing Bureau of European School Student Unions (OBESSU) also stipulates that all students should have a right to

  • a legally recognized school council
  • participate in important school decisions including the content of lectures, selection of texts, and teaching methods
  • appeal decisions they deem unfavorable
  • all normal civil rights
  • high-quality education (The Declaration of School Student Rights, 2014).

Similar rights are expressed in the Student Bill of Rights adopted by the United States Student Association (United States Student Association, 2012). The rights most commonly described in student bills of rights include the rights to free expression, safety and well-being, due process, personal learning, and privacy. Also often included are statements regarding the right to employability, civic participation, inclusion, fair assessment, and preparation for technology (Martin, 2015). Some of these rights are discussed below.

Recently, too, there has been increasing emphasis on privacy laws, many of which guarantee the individual’s right to confidentiality with respect to personal information. Determining whether this also includes test results is an important issue for educators.

 

The Right to Privacy

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA) is the U.S. federal law which protects the student’s right to privacy (U.S. Department of Education, 2018). Its main provisions are the following:

  • It gives students access to their education records.
  • It provides an opportunity for students to petition to have records changed if they think these are unfair or inaccurate.
  • It grants students some control over the disclosure of personal information from these records.

In practice, what the law means is that schools typically require written permission from students or their parents (or other adults legally responsible for them) to release to a third party any “protected” information from student records (“third party” does not include the student or the student’s parents).

Protected Information

Protected information includes educational information, directory information, and personally identifiable information, each of which is subject to different limitations. For example, personally identifiable information can only be disclosed after obtaining a signed document (from the parent or student if the student is over 18) that specifies to whom the information will be released and for what purpose.

In contrast, release of directory information, such as a list of student names and phone numbers, is not normally considered an invasion of privacy and is permitted under FERPA. However, the disclosure of this type of information is subject to the student’s or caregiver’s right to prohibit it (Hlavac and Easterly, 2015).

Formal Records Versus Informal Grading

This law applies to formal school records; however, it does not apply to informal grading in school. For example, a fourth-grade teacher is not in violation of privacy rights when he hangs an informal chart indicating the number of assignments turned in, or adheres stars on the chart to indicate how well the assignments were done.

Yet he would be in violation of privacy laws if he reported how Joshua did on his arithmetic test to, say, his Aunt Miri—unless she is his “authorized representative” as defined by the act (usually a parent or legal guardian), or unless the parents or legal guardians have granted express and informed consent that she be given that information.

And if Joshua were 18 or more, teachers and postsecondary educators would be in violation of his legal right to privacy were they to divulge, even to a parent, any protected information in his records without his consent.

Other Appropriate Uses of Information

The Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act does not require that school records, test results, and other private information be available only to teachers, school administrators, parents, and students. The law also stipulates that student information may be used for funding applications, research, or other legitimate purposes as long as a student identifier system protecting the individual identity of each student is used.

The results of various student assessments sometimes need to be shared among other individuals and institutions for what are considered legally defensible—and sometimes legally mandated—purposes. For example, educating children under the mandates of IDEA often requires that information that might otherwise be considered private and confidential be shared at least among all who are involved in developing individualized education plans (IEPs) for those children. That doing so is legitimate and legally defensible seems clear.

Sometimes less clear are the privacy rights surrounding the identification of children with special needs. Whenever someone is identified as being in need of special educational services, that person’s record is essentially being flagged and brought to the attention of a variety of people. This practice might have serious, life-changing consequences for the individual involved, and it might also be a violation of that person’s privacy rights. Schools need to be especially mindful of the possibility of misidentifying and mislabeling children.

Informed Consent

Informed consent implies three conditions:

  • that parents (or mature students) be fully informed about why and how personal information will be collected and used, or how and why research will be conducted;
  • that they have the capacity to refuse to participate or to accept; and
  • that accepting or refusing be entirely voluntary without fear of penalty or reprisal.

The notion of informed consent is especially relevant in educational and psychological research, much of which involves learners of different ages and characteristics. With respect to such research, both children and their parents have the right to be fully informed regarding the research. Furthermore, investigators must obtain both the child’s consent and the written permission of the parents or legal guardians.

1.11Self-Assessment

Focus Question What is a self-regulated learner?

When we’re children, our parents tell us how smart and nimble we are. They smile and clap their hands and say, “Wow, you speak so well!” It makes us proud 3-year-olds, and it reinforces the behaviors they praise.

Later when we go to school, teachers tell us how clever and witty we are. They mark our tests and clap their hands and say, “Wow! You draw such nice pictures . . . Oh, wait a minute, they’re not so good, your number skills.”

And somewhere along the road we ask ourselves, “How ethical and moral am I?” “How agile and alert?” “How bright?” Gradually, we learn to look at our behaviors, at our small accomplishments, at our failures. We discover how to compare our actions, our gifts, our failings, to those of others. We develop notions of how things should be or how we would like them to be. We build up personal standards that we can now use to become judges of our own strengths and weaknesses.

Thus, gradually, we learn the skills of self-assessment—the skills that allow us to evaluate our own abilities and accomplishments. Self-assessment is a fundamental aspect of learning how to learn. It is a cognitive strategy of the utmost importance. Self-assessment is fundamental to improvement.

In the same way that athletes have to be able to evaluate their performance to improve it, learners have to know how well or how badly their cognitive processes are functioning in order to get better.

Self-Regulated Learners

One of the important goals of schools is to teach students how to learn, to make of them self-regulated learners—learners who are autonomous in that:

  • they take responsibility for setting their own learning objectives,
  • they select and apply strategies to reach them, and
  • they are able to monitor and evaluate the effectiveness of their efforts.

Being a self-regulated learner requires developing the skills of metacognition—knowing about knowing (see Chapter 3). Metacognitive skills provide learners with ways of estimating the effects of their efforts, of developing strategies for learning and remembering, of problem solving, and of self-assessment. Research supports the notion that strong self-assessment skills contribute to effective learning (e.g., Maxlow, 2017; Sharma et al., 2016).

The Bases of Self-Assessment

Students’ self-assessments—their personal notions about their progress and achievements, and about their strengths and weaknesses—are based on a number of different factors.

For example, the information learners receive from peers and teachers about how well they are performing invariably colors their self-assessments. Some of this information is derived from the formal and informal assessment procedures that schools and teachers provide.

In addition, comparisons that students make between themselves and others is also a rich source of information about self-competence. Self-assessments may reflect a range of sometimes subtle influences, including personality characteristics. Some people are naturally confident, possess high opinions of themselves, and have a strong sense of their ability to succeed. Others, not so much.

One purpose of educational assessment is to foster the skills involved in self-assessment. Some students learn and use those skills exceptionally well; they have realistic appraisals of their abilities and their weaknesses, and they use their self-appraisals wisely to guide their efforts. Others are prone to self-assessments that exaggerate their gifts—or that depreciate them. Do those of us afflicted with poor self-assessment skills ever learn that we do not really know ourselves? 1.12What Works in Education?

Focus Question What works in the classroom?

There is no simple answer to what works in education. Historically, there have been a wide range of beliefs about what learning is and a sometimes bewildering assortment of recommendations about the best ways to go about the teaching/learning process. A huge amount of research has been undertaken to assess the value of these many recommendations. Although the results of this research have not always been clear and without controversy, recent summaries and analyses of this research have begun to answer the question, What works?

Formative Assessment and Feedback Work

What works, research informs us, is assessment that is designed for learning rather than simply of learning. It is assessment that is conducted for learners rather than simply to learners. In brief, it is formative assessment (e.g. Craig and Orland-Barak, 2015; Maxlow, 2017).

Good formative assessment reflects the processes involved in the backward design approach to curriculum and assessment shown in Figure 1.4, as well as the assessment cycle illustrated in Figure 1.5. That is, formative assessment requires:

  1. Knowing where you want learners to arrive (curriculum/lesson/unit goals)
  2. Finding or devising assessments to determine whether they have reached desired goals
  3. Planning and implementing appropriate learning experiences
  4. Using ongoing assessments to assist the learner and to modify instructional approaches.

Although teachers have traditionally assumed responsibility for each of these processes, current research suggests that learners need to be centrally involved at all stages.

This means that learners, like teachers, need to understand learning objectives, and they need to know what the criteria for success are. It also means that they need to be involved, in collaboration with their teachers and their peers, and in selecting and carrying out the learning tasks that will move them toward relevant instructional objectives.

 As we see in Chapter 5, feedback (information learners receives about their performance) is the cornerstones of formative assessment. One of the key factors in learner achievement has to do with the decisions that the teacher makes in the classroom on a minute-to-minute basis and on the assessment and learning strategies that are activated. Teachers have the professional responsibility to be aware of and use the best of all possible instructional practices. Formative assessment—assessment for learning—is one of those “best” practices.

Technology provides educators with new ways of packaging and delivering educational offerings, of developing and administering assessment procedures, and even of making new discoveries about what works.

Themes

Types of Assesment

Historically, the main purpose of educational assessment was to provide a grade (summative assessment); increasingly, assessment has become an integral part of the day-to-day functioning of schools, where it is used to provide feedback for guiding and improving teaching and learning (formative assessment: assessment for learning rather than assessment of learning.). Placement assessment gathers information useful for placing students in courses, programs, study groups, and so on. Performance-based assessment, unlike many paper-and-pencil or computer-based tests, asks learners to perform or construct what is being assessed. In education, tests are usually specific instruments or procedures used to measure achievement and progress; assessment is a more general term for all the activities involved in educational testing and measurement. Evaluation refers to making a value judgment or appraising something relative to certain criteria.

Curriculum Planning and Assessment

Students tend to study what teachers test and (hopefully) teach; hence the importance of educational assessment that measures the intentions (educational goals) of educational systems. The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) is the principal piece of legislation governing K-12 education in the U.S. It stipulates that all states must develop high educational standards (goals or objectives), and appropriate related assessments and approaches to instruction. The backward design approach to curriculum building (where the first step identifies standards (goals), the next relates to developing assessments to determine progress toward goals, and the third deals with instructional approaches) is highly useful in this regard. Collaboration, often in the form of professional learning communities (PLCs), is frequently involved at all stages of the curriculum building process including the sharing of standards and approaches to assessment and instruction. For assessment and instructional purposes, the most useful learning objectives are framed as highly explicit, observable intended outcomes of instructional activities.

The implications of Current Legislation

Mandates of ESSA relating to high standards, appropriate assessments, and optimal approaches to instruction, have led to increased emphasis on the use of common core standards across states, often developed by a consortium of educators. This legislation often results in the use of state-wide standardized tests, the results of which have important implications for learners, teachers, schools, and educational funding, leading to the label high-stakes testing. Assessment of teacher competence, often with thePraxis™ tests, is another form of high-stakes testing (as was assessment and funding based on the now terminated Race to the Top (R2T) program, which granted funds on the basis of school and teacher performance). The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) also has implications for testing. It outlines the school’s responsibilities with respect to providing services for children with special needs. It guarantees zero rejection, parent participation, education in the least restrictive environment, free and appropriate education, fair evaluation, and individual education plans (IEPs) for all learners with special needs.
